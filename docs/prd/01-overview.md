# Overview

## What We Are Building and Why

Interactive media has a ceiling that has never been broken. Stories are authored for a generic audience. Games are balanced for an average player. Every person who buys a ticket to the same movie sees the same film.

Tabletop roleplaying games cracked this problem decades ago. A skilled GM holds the arc in their head, reads every person at the table, and makes the session unrepeatable for that specific group on that specific night. The experience moves people because it was made for them, in real time, by a human who was paying attention. The problem is that this scales to almost nobody. It requires a trained facilitator, hours of preparation, and a group patient enough to learn a system before they can play.

Arcwright automates the facilitation layer without removing human authorship. The arc is still designed by a person: the plot skeleton, the characters, the world rules, the constraints on what can and cannot happen. The engine makes that arc alive for the specific group inside it, reading who is in the room and producing an experience that could not have happened with any other combination of people. Every session is unrepeatable. No GM required.

The commercial opportunity is not just party games. The same infrastructure that runs a murder mystery also runs a corporate team-building event, a museum installation, a language-learning simulation, or a monster-catching RPG with procedurally-generated worlds. This is platform territory. But platforms without proof are vaporware. The games are the proof: each one stress-tests the engine against a genuinely different design problem, and each one ships as a real product with real players.

Arcwright builds both. The platform is the primary commercial product. The games are how the platform earns the right to exist.

## North Star Vision

Arcwright's maturity unfolds in three phases, each of which earns the right to the next. Phase gates are proof signals, not calendar dates.

**Horizon 1 (target: 6-12 months): Creative studio with internal infrastructure.** The company looks like Pixar: a creative studio that built its own technology because nothing off the shelf could do what it needed. Nightcap ships as a real product. The Arcwright engine powers it. The platform is not yet available to external developers; it is proven in production by the company's own work. The team is effectively one person.

**Horizon 2 (target: 12-24 months): Studio plus platform.** The company looks like early Valve: still making excellent games, but the engine is becoming its own thing. The monster RPG ships. The enterprise team-building product enters the market. A small closed beta opens to external developers who have been specifically recruited, not a public API launch. The first co-founder or senior hire joins, in whichever role is most painfully absent when the H1 proof signals hit.

**Horizon 3+ (target: 24+ months): Platform-first with active studio.** The company looks like Stripe, but the creative studio never shuts down. The platform is the primary revenue engine. External developers ship experiences on Arcwright that Arcwright did not build. The studio continues producing games that set the quality bar and push the platform's capability ceiling. The games are now also the best marketing the platform has.

At full maturity: Arcwright is the default infrastructure layer for developers building adaptive experiences, regardless of genre. The company is known among developers for the platform and among players for the games. Neither identity cannibalizes the other.
