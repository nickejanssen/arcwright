## The One-Liner

**Arcwright makes stories that are alive.** Human-authored worlds that
run — that know who's in the room, remember what happened, and never
play the same night twice.

## The Problem With Stories

Every story humanity has ever shipped is frozen. The novel doesn't
know you're reading it. The film plays the same for an empty theater.
Even games — the most interactive medium ever built — ship as
authored trees: bigger trees, better hidden, still trees. The
industry's answer to "make it feel alive" has been to spend more on
the tree.

And the new answer — point a language model at players and let it
improvise — produces the opposite of story: no author, no structure,
no stakes that hold, contradictions on night two. Slop with a
personality. The industry is currently choosing between cathedrals
and fog.

## The Thesis

The choice is false. A story has two layers, and they want different
machines.

**Structure is human.** The arc, the beats, the characters that
matter, what must happen, what may never happen — that is authorship,
and no model replaces it, and we never ask one to.

**Performance is runtime.** Which night this is, who showed up, what
they said, what the room needs next — that is execution, and it is
finally automatable with full coherence, because we made coherence
infrastructure: every session runs on a knowledge graph that records
who knows what, when they learned it, and from whom, and no character
— human or AI — can speak outside what they know.

Arcwright is the layer between the author and the room. Layer 1 is
the world someone wrote. Layer 3 is whatever screen or speaker is
nearby. **We are Layer 2: the narrative runtime.** Authors write
worlds once; the runtime performs them forever, differently, for
everyone, without breaking them.

## Why This Is A Company And Not A Feature

Because every layer of it compounds:

- **The engine** is genre-agnostic and surface-agnostic by
  architecture, not aspiration — the same infrastructure that lets a
  murder suspect lie coherently lets a heist crew compartmentalize a
  plan or a raid boss hold a grudge. One engine, every genre, any
  screen.
- **The games** are the proof and the wedge. We ship our own first —
  living-room experiences where the platform's headline primitive is
  the *gameplay* (characters who remember are characters you can catch
  lying) — because the fastest way to prove a runtime is to run it.
- **The platform** is the endgame. When authoring an arc is as
  structured as we've made it — a premise, a beat spine, an ownership
  table, rails, voices — then every studio, every IP holder, every
  writer with a world in a drawer is a customer. The world's best
  stories are sitting in frozen media. We are how they wake up.

And underneath: unit economics designed on day one, not discovered in
a postmortem — task-routed models, cached context, small models for
machinery and quality-tier models only where the audience can hear the
difference. Alive doesn't have to mean expensive. That discipline is a
moat, because our competitors will discover it as a crisis.

## What We Believe

1. **Authors are not replaceable. Performances are.** AI that writes
   your story is a commodity. AI that *performs* your story, under
   your rules, without ever contradicting itself — that's an industry.
2. **Coherence is the product.** Anyone can generate content. Memory,
   provenance, and consequence are what make content matter. We made
   them infrastructure.
3. **The room is the platform.** The most underserved gaming surface
   on earth is a couch with four people on it. We start there — not
   because it's small, but because it's honest: a living room notices
   instantly when a story isn't alive.
4. **Determinism is drama's best friend.** Every reveal is earned
   because every fact was fixed before the first line was spoken. The
   machine never decides what happened; it decides how tonight gets
   told.

## The Arc (Ours)

Prove it in one living room. Then every living room. Then every
author. The first game teaches the engine, the engine attracts the
authors, the authors become the catalog, and the catalog is the
platform nobody can copy — because you can copy a model, but you
cannot copy a library of living worlds and the runtime they trust.

Stories have been frozen for five thousand years.
We're the thaw.
